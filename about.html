About Accessibility

Understanding why the web must be built for everyone — and what standards guide that goal.

The Case for Accessibility

Why It Matters

Digital accessibility ensures that websites, tools, and technologies are designed and developed so that people with disabilities can use them. When we build accessibly, we build for everyone.

Over 1 billion people worldwide — about 15% of the global population — live with some form of disability. These include visual, auditory, motor, cognitive, and speech impairments. When digital experiences aren't accessible, these individuals are effectively excluded from modern life.

Beyond moral obligation, accessibility is increasingly a legal requirement. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), Section 508, and the EU Web Accessibility Directive all mandate digital access for public-facing and government sites.

⚖️ Legal Compliance

ADA lawsuits related to web inaccessibility exceeded 4,000 cases in 2023 alone. Compliance protects organizations from costly litigation.

💼 Business Impact

The disability market represents over $13 trillion in disposable income globally. Accessible sites reach a wider audience and improve conversion.

🔍 SEO Benefits

Many accessibility practices — semantic HTML, alt text, descriptive links — directly improve search engine optimization and page rankings.

Standards & Guidelines

WCAG Explained

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), published by the W3C, are the internationally recognized standard for web accessibility.

A

Level A — Minimum

The bare minimum for accessibility. Failing Level A criteria creates significant barriers for some users with disabilities. Examples: all non-text content has alt text, no keyboard traps exist.

AA

Level AA — Standard

The most widely referenced conformance level and the legal target in most regulations. Covers contrast ratios (4.5:1), captions, consistent navigation, and visible focus indicators.

AAA

Level AAA — Enhanced

The highest level of accessibility, not typically required in full but recommended where feasible. Includes 7:1 contrast ratio, sign language interpretation, and extended audio description.

2.2

WCAG 2.2 — Latest

Released in 2023, WCAG 2.2 adds 9 new success criteria, focusing on users with cognitive disabilities and mobile users. Key additions include visible focus and consistent help mechanisms.

Who We Design For

Types of Disabilities

Accessibility covers a spectrum of disabilities, each with unique design considerations.

👁️

Visual

Includes blindness, low vision, and color blindness. Users may rely on screen readers, magnification software, or high-contrast modes. Solutions: alt text, semantic HTML, sufficient contrast, scalable text.

👂

Auditory

Includes deafness and hearing impairment. Users cannot rely on audio. Solutions: captions for video, transcripts for audio, visual alternatives to audio cues.

🖐️

Motor

Includes conditions affecting fine motor control, such as tremors, paralysis, or repetitive strain. Users may use keyboard only, switch access, or eye-tracking. Solutions: keyboard navigation, large targets, no time limits.

🧠

Cognitive

Includes dyslexia, ADHD, autism, and memory impairments. Users benefit from clear language, consistent layouts, and minimal distractions. Solutions: plain language, predictable navigation, sufficient time.

🗣️

Speech

Affects voice input and output. Users may rely on text-based communication. Solutions: ensure voice input is not the only interaction method; provide text-based alternatives to all voice interfaces.

⚡

Situational

Temporary or situational disabilities affect everyone: a broken arm, bright sunlight, noisy environment, or slow internet. Accessible design benefits all users in all contexts.

Common Problems & Fixes

Top Accessibility Failures

According to WebAIM's Million Report, these are the most common WCAG failures found on the web's top million pages.

🚫

Missing Alt Text on Images

Screen readers skip images with no alt attribute, leaving blind users without context. Decorative images should use alt=""; informative images need descriptive text.

Fix: alt="Descriptive text" or alt="" for decorative
🚫

Insufficient Color Contrast

Light gray text on white background fails WCAG 1.4.3. Affects users with low vision, older users, and anyone in bright sunlight. Minimum ratio: 4.5:1 for normal text.

Fix: Use a contrast checker; minimum 4.5:1 ratio
🚫

Empty or Missing Form Labels

Forms with only placeholder text — or labels not programmatically associated with inputs — prevent screen readers from identifying field purpose when focused.

Fix: <label for="id"> linked to <input id="id">
🚫

No Visible Focus Indicator

CSS that removes the default focus outline (outline: none) makes keyboard navigation impossible for sighted users who tab through pages.

Fix: Never remove :focus styles; design a custom visible indicator
🚫

Empty Link Text

Links with only an icon, image, or "Click here" text provide no context for screen reader users navigating a list of links on a page.

Fix: Descriptive link text or aria-label on icon links
🚫

Missing Document Language

Without lang on the <html> element, screen readers may use the wrong voice profile, mispronouncing words and confusing users.

Fix: <html lang="en">
